ABSTRACT 

 

According to the World Health Organization (WHO) today there are more than 285 million visually impaired people in the world, who still use traditional tools like canes, guide dogs, and sticks[1]. EchoNav is designed for people who are unable to see and cannot recognize objects based on their shape and size. They also don’t know how to use certain objects or what their names are. EchoNav solves all these problems through voice-based assistance[2][3].It helps them move from one place to another using AR-assistance, a smart navigation system, handsfree experience, real-time audio feedback, and smart camera handling — all of which we provide through EchoNav[4[5][6].To develop EchoNav, components like the TF Luna LiDAR sensor, servo motor, Raspberry Pi 3B+, and pyttsx3 speech engine have been used[7][8][9]. The navigation system uses AR navigation, which provides hands-free and real-time audio feedback[4][6].The LiDAR sensor and servo motor are used to create a 2D map that detects surrounding objects[10][11]. All this data is processed through the Raspberry Pi 3B+, which serves as the main operational unit of the system[8]. The processed data is then delivered to visually impaired users in the form of voice alerts[2][3].Any data received in text format is converted into audio/speech using the pyttsx3 engine[9]. We have provided 180 degree camera visibility in this system and have also added ray based object detection feature[12][13]. Ray based object detection means that if the ray falls on any object, it does not bypass it and some kind of shape is formed, hence it finds the object through the ray. 

EchoNav provides a cost-effective, real-time assistive solution that can be further enhanced with features like GPS integration, voice commands, and cloud connectivity for future smart navigation systems.

Chapter: 1 

Introduction 

 

1.1 Background 

Human mobility is an essential part of daily life that most people take for granted. The ability to move freely and safely from one place to another enables independence, productivity, and social interaction. However, for individuals who are blind or visually impaired (BVI), even basic navigation can be a daunting and potentially dangerous task. Crossing a busy street, avoiding obstacles in a crowded room, or simply walking through an unfamiliar building becomes challenging without access to reliable visual information. 

Globally, the World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that over 285 million people live with some form of visual impairment. Out of these, at least 39 million are completely blind. In India alone, the National Programme for Control of Blindness (NPCB) reports over 12 million blind individuals, making it one of the countries with the highest number of visually impaired people in the world. This widespread challenge calls for technological solutions that can empower the visually impaired to navigate the world with confidence and safety. 

Historically, tools such as the white cane have played a pivotal role in aiding mobility. Introduced in the early 20th century, the white cane allows users to detect objects on the ground within their immediate surroundings. However, it has several limitations—it cannot detect overhead obstacles, does not offer any directional guidance, and requires physical contact with objects before detection. Moreover, it offers no insight into what the object is—whether it’s a person, a wall, or a stairway. 

Another option available to visually impaired individuals is the guide dog, a specially trained service animal that helps its owner navigate environments safely. While guide dogs can be incredibly helpful, they are expensive to train and maintain, and not everyone is comfortable relying on animals for mobility. Moreover, guide dogs are not widely accessible in countries with limited infrastructure and support for disability services. 

With the emergence of advanced sensor technologies, embedded systems, and machine learning, there is a growing opportunity to create more intelligent and responsive mobility aids. Devices equipped with LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging), computer vision, and 3D audio feedback can now interpret the environment in real-time and provide meaningful alerts to users without relying on sight. This is the space where EchoNav enters—the proposed smart navigation system for real-time obstacle detection and audio-assisted mobility. 

EchoNav is envisioned as a wearable or mountable device that uses real-time distance sensors, camera-based object classification, and audio feedback to provide spatial awareness. It eliminates the need for physical probing or constant human guidance. The system can inform users about their surroundings through directional audio alerts such as “Obstacle on your left—1 meter away” or “Stairs ahead—proceed with caution.” Unlike conventional aids, EchoNav operates autonomously, adapts to indoor and outdoor settings, and minimizes dependence on internet connectivity or mobile phones. 

The evolution of smart assistive technologies for the blind reflects broader advances in artificial intelligence, edge computing, and low-power electronics. Today’s microcontrollers like the Raspberry Pi, combined with open-source frameworks, allow developers to build powerful realtime systems at a fraction of the cost of commercial devices. Innovations in LiDAR, once used only in autonomous cars, now offer centimetre-level precision for consumer-level projects. 

The EchoNav project builds upon this technological foundation to bridge the gap between affordability, functionality, and ease of use. It is designed not just as a product, but as a platform—one that can be improved, customized, and adapted to diverse user needs. Its core strength lies in combining multiple sensory inputs into a coherent output that the user can understand easily and act upon. 

In short, the need for smart mobility aids has never been greater. Whether it is for educational, professional, or personal life, independent mobility is a right—not a privilege. EchoNav stands as a step toward making that right a reality for millions of individuals worldwide. 

 

	1.2 Problem Statement 	 

Despite significant technological progress in recent decades, there remains a huge gap in the accessibility of advanced mobility tools for the visually impaired. The problem is not the lack of ideas, but rather the lack of affordable, reliable, and adaptable solutions that can be deployed at scale and used effectively in real-world conditions. 

Let us analyse the challenges across a few key dimensions: 

1. LackOfObjectRecognition 

Most existing navigation aids detect the presence of objects, but do not identify what they are. Knowing that an object is in front is not as helpful as knowing it is “a person,” “a plastic chair,” or “a staircase.” This distinction is vital in determining how to respond to the obstacle. For example, a user may choose to wait for a person to pass, but might decide to step over a chair or walk around it. Systems that lack classification are inherently limited in functionality. 

2. UnreliableFeedbackMechanisms 

Devices that use vibration or light indicators may not provide enough context for users to make safe decisions. Audio-based cues are more natural, especially for the visually impaired, but they must be spatially aligned and given in real-time. Many existing tools fail to provide timely or intelligible feedback, leading to confusion or errors in judgment. 

3. DependenceOnNetworkOrInfrastructure 

A large number of smart navigation systems rely on GPS, Wi-Fi, or cloud-based processing to function effectively. This creates a dependency that limits usage in areas with poor network connectivity, such as rural regions, underground structures, or large buildings. 

4. HighCostAndComplexity 

Assistive devices that integrate computer vision or LiDAR are often priced in the range of ₹50,000 to ₹2, 00,000, making them unaffordable for most users, especially in developing countries. Moreover, these devices require training, calibration, or specialized support, further complicating their adoption. 

5. IncompatibilityWithEverydayUse 

Some devices are too bulky, require handheld control, or drain battery quickly. They are not designed with the user’s comfort and daily routines in mind. For technology to be genuinely assistive, it must be invisible in its presence but invaluable in its impact. 

These limitations collectively create an ecosystem where the blind and visually impaired are either forced to rely on outdated tools or invest in expensive alternatives that may not be suitable for their context. 

EchoNav’s mission is to challenge these norms. By focusing on real-time obstacle detection, object classification, and intuitive 3D audio cues, it offers a low-cost, modular, and offlinecapable solution. It uses servo-driven LiDAR for wide-area scanning, lightweight computer vision models for object recognition, and text-to-speech engines to provide immediate verbal alerts. All of this is powered by an onboard processor like the Raspberry Pi, making it portable, reliable, and scalable. 

 

 

 

1.3 Objectives 

The EchoNav system has been conceptualized with the aim of solving real-world challenges faced by visually impaired individuals in both indoor and outdoor environments. The objectives are not arbitrary — each one targets a specific limitation found in existing navigation tools, and together, they form a cohesive, integrated solution that balances cost, functionality, and usability. 

Let us look at the objectives in detail: 

 

1.3.1 To develop a wide-angle obstacle detection system using LiDAR and servo rotation 

Traditional proximity sensors like ultrasonic modules offer only point-to-point data and are often affected by the material and angle of surfaces. EchoNav overcomes this by implementing LiDAR-based scanning with a rotating servo. The system continuously rotates the LiDAR sensor across a 180-degree arc in front of the user, thereby capturing environmental depth data across a wide field of view. 

This enables the device to construct a real-time spatial map of obstacles. 

Compared to fixed sensors, the rotating LiDAR offers more accurate, dynamic coverage and can detect objects at various heights and angles. 

The output is fast, accurate, and non-intrusive. 

 

1.3.2 To implement a lightweight object classification system using embedded AI 

Merely detecting objects is not enough. EchoNav incorporates a machine learning-based classifier (e.g., Mobile Net or YOLO-tiny models) to identify and label commonly encountered obstacles such as: 

People 

Staircases 

Poles 

Bags 

Furniture 

Vehicles 

These models are optimized to run on Raspberry Pi with limited compute power and deliver real-time classification. This allows the system to say: “Person on right at 1.2 meters” instead of a generic “Obstacle ahead.” 

 

1.3.3 To deliver real-time, spatially-aware audio feedback via text-to-speech (TTS) 

To make the device intuitive and easy to understand, EchoNav uses 3D audio cues generated through text-to-speech technology. 

It provides natural language feedback like “Wall on your left—very close” or “Empty path ahead.” 

The alerts are timed and directionally accurate, helping the user navigate without confusion. 

This method is non-intrusive and works in both noisy and silent environments. 

 

1.3.4 To design a portable, wearable, and hands-free system 

The proposed system is wearable — either mounted on the user's belt or integrated into clothing or a shoulder strap. This avoids the need for handheld devices or distracting equipment. 

Weight is kept under 500 grams 

The design ensures freedom of hand movement 

The system is unobtrusive and visually minimal 

 

1.3.5 To build the system at a cost below ₹5,000 

Affordability is a major design constraint. All components are selected based on: 

Availability in local and online Indian markets 

Compatibility with open-source platforms 

Cost-effectiveness without sacrificing performance 

This ensures the device can be mass-produced or DIY-built by individuals or NGOs without high capital. 

 

1.3.6 To ensure offline functionality and modular design 

The system is designed to work without the internet. All object detection and feedback logic runs locally on the Raspberry Pi. Also: 

Each module (LiDAR, camera, TTS) is plug-and-play 

Future upgrades can include GPS, haptic feedback, or Bluetooth pairing with phones 

This makes EchoNav scalable and future-ready. 

 

1.4 Scope of the Project 

The scope of EchoNav extends across hardware, software, and human factors. It is not merely a prototype but a proof of concept for how modern embedded intelligence can assist real-life challenges. 

 

1.4.1 Hardware Scope The system includes: 

LiDAR sensor: Used for precise distance measurement 

Servo motor: Enables LiDAR rotation for area scanning 

Camera module: Captures real-time images for object classification 

Headphones or speakers: Deliver TTS feedback 

Raspberry Pi: Central controller for data processing 

Power bank: Ensures mobility 

The goal is to integrate all components into a compact form factor that can be worn easily and charged like a mobile phone. 

 

1.4.2 Software Scope 

On the software front, EchoNav employs: 

Python scripts for sensor control 

ML libraries (Tensor Flow Lite, OpenCV) for image processing 

Distance thresholding algorithms 

Angle-based orientation for feedback direction 

Text-to-speech library for voice output (e.g., pyttsx3, gTTS) 

All code is written to be modular, readable, and upgradeable by future developers or researchers. 

 

1.4.3 Functional Scope 

EchoNav is suitable for: 

Indoor navigation (homes, schools, offices, hospitals) 

Outdoor use (markets, streets, bus stops) 

Environments with partial or no light 

 	Day or night conditions  	Places with moving or stationary obstacles

Currently, GPS-based long-distance navigation is not integrated. 

Weatherproofing is limited; the system is not waterproof. 

It is designed for adult users with moderate tech familiarity. 

Does not support haptic/vibration feedback in the current version.

1.5 Need for the System 

We live in a world where visual information dominates design—from signage and traffic lights to building layouts. Unfortunately, this visual bias excludes those who cannot see or interpret these cues. Although awareness about inclusive design is growing, implementation is far from complete. 

Let’s explore why EchoNav is not just a helpful innovation, but a necessary one: 

 

1.5.1 Rising Visual Impairment Worldwide 

WHO reports say uncorrected visual impairment is among the top 10 causes of disability globally. 

Aging populations and diabetes-related blindness are increasing. 

Children born with blindness in underprivileged areas often remain uneducated due to mobility limitations. 

 

1.5.2 Accessibility in Developing Countries 

In India, affordable assistive technology is hard to find. 

Government schemes rarely reach rural disabled citizens. 

Urban infrastructure (e.g., metros, banks, malls) often lacks tactile paths, audio guides, or braille. 

EchoNav fills this accessibility void by acting as a “digital eye” for the blind. 

 

1.5.3 Lack of Affordable and Smart Alternatives 

The existing devices in the market are either too costly, too bulky, or too complex. 

Most do not offer intelligent feedback, only warning tones or buzzers. 

Even smart glasses and AI-based devices are out of reach for the average consumer. 

 

 

 

 

1.5.4 Technological Readiness 

We now have open-source tools, low-cost hardware, and community support that can create mass solutions. 

EchoNav is a Do-It-Yourself–friendly platform that schools, colleges, and NGOs can use. 

It also acts as a foundation for academic research and future development in assistive tech. 

 

1.5.5 Human Impact 

With EchoNav, a visually impaired person can walk independently to a bus stop, avoid street obstacles, or navigate a college campus without relying on anyone. 

It boosts confidence, dignity, and freedom — the three pillars of human-centered design. 

For caregivers and family members, it offers peace of mind, knowing the user is safer and more aware of their surroundings.

2.1 Introduction 

1. Object Detection and Vision-based Models 

Object detection is used to identify obstacles and significant objects present in the environment, which may vary in size and appearance. Redmon and Farhadi (2018) introduced the YOLOv3 algorithm, a real-time object detection system known for its good speed and moderate accuracy. It uses a single neural network for both bounding box prediction and object classification, making it ideal for embedded systems [1]. 

Later, YOLOv4 was introduced by Bochkovskiy et al. (2020), which improved upon YOLOv3 in terms of speed, accuracy, and reliability. It incorporated features like Spatial Pyramid Pooling, Cross-Stage Partial connections, and Self-Adversarial Training, making it suitable for lowlatency applications [2]. YOLOv4-Tiny, a lightweight open-source version, is fast and requires less memory, allowing it to run efficiently on low-resource devices like Raspberry Pi [20]. Considering this, I have used Raspberry Pi in my device to customize it according to my design requirements. 

 

2. SLAM and Monocular Navigation 

Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) techniques are used to create real-time 3D maps of the surrounding space, allowing users to better understand their environment. LSDSLAM, developed by Engel et al. (2014), enables 3D reconstruction using a monocular camera and is now being applied in various settings. Although slightly computation-heavy, it significantly enhances navigation accuracy in dynamic environments [3]. 3D mapping also allows for virtual object placement and navigation. 

 

3. Sensor-Based Obstacle Detection 

Ultrasonic and LiDAR sensors are commonly used for obstacle detection across different object types and sizes. Ashwin Kumar et al. (2015) demonstrated that ultrasonic sensors can be effectively integrated into wearable devices to provide real-time proximity feedback for blind users [4], eliminating the need for traditional sticks or guide dogs. Additionally, LiDAR Lite v3, documented by SparkFun, offers accurate distance measurement through laser pulses, especially useful in outdoor or low-light conditions [11]. For this reason, I have also incorporated LiDAR sensors into my device. 

Harish and Mohan (2021) developed a smart guidance system by integrating ultrasonic sensors with microcontrollers. This solution is both cost-effective and practical for visually impaired users [16]. 

 

4. Embedded Systems and IoT Integration 

Modern assistive devices are mostly based on embedded platforms, enhancing speed and effectiveness. The Raspberry Pi 4 Model B features a quad-core processor and supports various peripherals, making it easier to integrate vision, speech, and sensor modules [12]. Additionally, Arduino microcontrollers are widely used for real-time sensor integration [19]. 

Kumar and Singh (2020) designed an IoT-enabled smart walking stick using sensors and cloud communication, significantly improving accessibility for the visually impaired [17]. This was a notable innovation in 2020. 

 

5. Text-to-Speech and Audio Feedback 

Audio feedback plays a vital role in conveying spatial information to visually impaired users. Python libraries like pyttsx3 [10] and eSpeak [13] are commonly used to generate offline voice output. Pyttsx3 is platform-independent, and eSpeak is popular due to its small size and opensource nature, making both suitable for embedded speech applications.

2.5.3 LiDAR-Based Systems 

Recent advancements in LiDAR sensors have enabled more precise obstacle detection. However, LiDAR is still rarely used in low-cost academic projects due to expense and integration challenges. LiDAR systems use a laser, a GPS, and an IMU to estimate the height of objects on the ground. This allows for accurate assessment of the size and location of objects. 

Differentiated LiDAR data is generated from waveforms—each point represents the peak energy points of the returned signal, which helps in obtaining precise information about the objects. 

These differentiated LiDAR points contain x, y, and z values. The z value is used to determine the height of objects, enabling accurate height estimation. 

LiDAR data can be used in various ways to estimate the height of objects and even the canopy cover of vegetation or trees. 

One notable project, “BLINDAID” (2021), used a 2D LiDAR module and stereo vision to detect the depth of obstacles, but the system was still dependent on a mobile app and required indoor calibration.

3.2 System Overview

4.5 Servo Scanning and Integration with LiDAR 

The servo motor is designed so that the LiDAR can cover a wider field of view (FOV) rather than being at a fixed angle — like a rotating radar. 

 

4.5.1 Servo Scanning Logic: 

Python import pigpio import time 

 

servo_pin = 18 pi = pigpio.pi() angle_range = [0, 180] 

 

def set_angle(angle): 

pulsewidth = int((angle / 180.0) * 1000 + 1000) pi.set_servo_pulsewidth(servo_pin, pulsewidth) 

 

while True:     for angle in range(0, 180, 15): 

set_angle(angle) time.sleep(0.2)     for angle in range(180, 0, -15): 

set_angle(angle) time.sleep(0.2) 

Scans from 0° to 180° and back. 

Every 15° it pauses and lets LiDAR collect distance data. 

This creates a fan-like 2D scan, which can be visualized as a radar sweep. 

 

4.5.2 LiDAR Data Capture (lidar_reader.py) 

import serial 

 

lidar = serial.Serial('/dev/ttyUSB0', 115200) 

 

def get_distance():     if lidar.in_waiting:         data = lidar.read(9)         if is_valid(data):             return parse_distance(data) 

Data is filtered with Kalman filter or moving average to reduce noise. 

Invalid spikes (distance >12m or <10cm) are discarded. 

Every scan mapped to servo angle to create polar map. 

 

4.6 Multithreading: Ensuring Parallel Execution 

Python's GIL (Global Interpreter Lock) could have been a bottleneck, so multithreading was fully implemented. 

4.6.1 Why Multithreading? 

Real-time performance keliye: 

Thread 1: Camera detection 

Thread 2: LiDAR + servo scan 

Thread 3: Audio feedback 

Thread 4: Button or voice command listener 

 

4.6.2 Sample Thread Structure 

import threading 

 

threading.Thread(target=run_camera).start() threading.Thread(target=run_lidar).start() threading.Thread(target=audio_output).start() 

Each module communicates via shared variables (with locks) to avoid race conditions.
